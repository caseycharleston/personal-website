## What's a yearly theme?
To explain a what a yearly theme is, first we should talk about new years resolutions. A new years resolution is typically something specific a person wants to accomplish by year's end. For example, "I want to read 1 book a week" or "I want to lose 50 lbs this year."

A yearly theme, put simply, is more general resolution. A theme is flexible and can apply to many areas of your life. For example, a reframing of the above resolutions are:
- Year of Reading: I want to accomplish more reading this year.
- Year of Fitness: I want to get into better shape this year.
The key observation here is that themes have no specific goal. Instead, they serve as a north star to guide your actions towards following your theme. If Year of Reading is your theme, you might be on your phone and choose to read a newsletter or article instead of scrolling through social media. Fitness isn't just about losing weight, it can also be about learning to cook and consistently meal prepping.

## My 2026 Yearly Theme
My yearly theme is the Year of Investment. I am about to graduate from university and I want to invest more into my future this year. Not only am I graduating, but I am moving to a new city in a new state. Every move comes with initial costs in regards to finding housing, routes to work, and new doctors to see. Given that, there are a few things I want to "invest" in this year:
- **Investing.** I am already invested in the stock market, but I want to setup more consistent cash flow into my trading account. Right now, I just randomly add more money if I see something  I want to buy. I also need to setup 401(k) contributions and plan to max out my Roth IRA. Both of these are related to retirement and it's always best to start as early as possible.
- **Finances**. Getting my first full-time job will mean I need to learn how to manage my money. Part of this is budgeting my monthly take-home pay to ensure I pay off any expenses and have money left for savings and investing.
- **Health.** I want to get better at taking care of myself. That means more exercising and eating better. I do still want to explore new restaurants, but I should account for those additional calories into my diet. A huge part of this is my interest in meal prepping food. (put the insta guy who does meal prep videos here)
- **Routine.** I have some routines I loosely follow and I want to become more consistent. This somewhat ties in to health and finances with keeping track of my eating habits, exercising, and expenses.

But really, a lot of things can be seen as an investment, that is what makes yearly themes so great. In my last semester, I have hung out a lot more with friends than any previous semester. You can interpret that as an investment into my social relationships. 

By year's end, I hope to be establishing better habits to set myself up for success in the future. I want to build the foundation of strong habits that will carry me to my desired end result: healthy finances and a healthy lifestyle.
 
## A personal yearly theme lookback
I've actually talked about this [before](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/caseycharleston_your-theme-activity-7149852201497022464-pbcc?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADrs4CMBl9hi7pRKG_JiE2iLxlpqC9eTKEM) on LinkedIn a while back. I've tried to do a yearly theme for a while now. 2023 was "Year of the Routine," 2024 was "Year of the Early Riser," and 2025 "Year of Data." Each one of these themes was successful for a few months, but I always let other responsibilities get in the way. I tried a few things to remedy this:
- Data logging to track theme progress. However, I never got into the habit of logging consistently.
- I even made my 2025 theme around data logging. Again, when I got busy, I never stuck with the habit.
- And now for this year: I've attempted to massively oversimplify the theme tracking with Things 3 and Obsidian.

Before I get into my new system, let me give some context. While a yearly theme has no metric of success, which is the point, I personally like tracking whether or not I'm following my theme. So for example, during the "Year of Data" I tracked how often I would log all of the data I wanted to track (I know, data about collecting data sounds crazy). This year, I've made a few changes to massively oversimplify this tracking and make it more forgiving.

## Todoist
I love a good todo list. It drives everything I do. I seriously don't know how people without one function (which is surprisingly common).

For the past three and a half years, Todoist has been my daily task manager. I loved the date entry natural language. You can put things like "every two weeks on Friday until May 5" and Todoist will automatically schedule it. I eventually got tired of Todoist for one main reason: it runs on a subscription model. Around the time I first subscribed to Todoist Pro, they announced an increase in the price to $60/year. They did allow current subscribers to continue paying the old price of $36/year, including me. However, they recently announced that pro-only features made after Dec 2025 will no longer be available to legacy pro users. I honestly find this baffling as the whole point of a legacy tier is to maintain goodwill with longstanding customers. After they abandoned legacy users, I wanted to ditch Todoist.
## A new todo list (actually two new ones)
Before the start of 2026, I really wanted to transition to a new system before school started up again. I researched multiple to do list apps and came to better understand my task tracking needs. I have three major types of tasks:
- Project tasks: One-and-done tasks working towards a discrete goal. Something like homework and coding projects fit into this. 
- Upkeep tasks: Tasks that are performed on a regular basis to maintain something. An example is something like keeping in touch with friends, paying bills, or synchronizing people at work.
- Habit tasks: These could be considered a subset of upkeep tasks. These are maintenance tasks that I do for my own benefit. Things like exercising, weekly task reviews, time tracking, and calorie counting are great examples.

Todoist is great at the first type. Everything is organized as a project. It's also okay for upkeep tasks, thought it is weird to consider "do your laundry" as a task under a project. Habit tasks is where it really falls apart. The #1 thing I need for habits is to track how often it is followed. Todoist has no native way of tracking when I completed or failed to complete a task. Additionally, habit tasks are not distinct from other task types in Todoist. The mental load of a project or upkeep task is much higher than that of a habit. Habits to me are supposed to be a glorified checkmark: you either did it or you didn't. By having them mixed in with other task types, it makes it seem like I have more work to do.

So then, what am I using now?
- Things 3 - for project and upkeep tasks
- HabitKit - for habit tasks

Let's talk cost. Things 3 on Mac is $50, and $10 on the iPhone. HabitKit is iPhone-only and costs $30. These are all one time costs, totaling $90. While that sounds like a lot, I've already paid $144 to use Todoist for four years of subscription. Were I have been on the new pricing, it would be $240. 

### Things 3
Things 3 is highly regarded as a super simple and easy to use to do list app. Here is what my Things 3 looks like:
![](posts/images/yearly-theme-discussion-today-view.png)

The UI is incredibly clean. Projects exist in Things are are great for project tasks. Areas are used to group projects and tasks associated ith them. In the 2026 - Year of Investment, notice I have both types: setting up a google sheet is a one-and-done project task, and my monthly review is a recurring upkeep task.

The key thing here is there are no habit tasks in my main to do list app. I also set a rule to avoid daily repeating tasks, as any task that must be done daily is essentially a habit.

### HabitKit
I've tried to use a few other habit tracking apps but none really had exactly what I needed. All I want is a glorified checklist of habits and some basic tracking to see how I'm doing. HabitKit is perfect:
![](posts/images/yearly-theme-discussion-habitkit-1.png)
 Each square serves as a simple checkmark to indicate if I completed that habit today or not. It's easy to customize and add new habits. As a bonus, if a habit is something I can complete retroactively, then I can easily check it off in the past week. Any of the unfilled squares is fillable with just a tap.

The data tracking is great as well. If I click on a habit, I can view my progress over the past few months and for the current month:
![](posts/images/yearly-theme-discussion-habitkit-data.png)
