# Favorite Games

## Disco Elysium

This game has been sitting in my library for a while and a friend urged me to play it. What can I say? It is an incredible game with an intriguing narrative and world. The art direction and writing are top-notch. By far my favorite character is your lieutenant sidekick, Kim Kitsuragi. His seriousness interspersed with his dry humor is such a perfect combination. The story beats and the dialogue are so intriguing, there were multiple times where I was about to put the game down for the day, and then a new bit of dialogue would keep me hooked for another hour.

The game itself is a narrative-driven detective role-playing game. You play as a detective who just woke up in a trashed apartment with alcohol-induced amnesia. You can choose what type of detective to be and are accompanied with 16 manifestations of your brain, all with competing agendas that at times butt heads. I will talk more about this later.

By far, the most memorable part of the game is the world building. The game is set in the fiction city of Revachol, with French connotations throughout, though the French do not exist in this game (thank god). The setting is in a 1950s Earth-adjacent world. A major part of the game is relearning the setting you find yourself in, as the amnesia your character strips them of all previously held knowledge. Because of that, the game pushes you to ask basic questions about the the city, the people, and the world, and even who you are as a person.

The main way to progress the game is through skill checks, powered by one of 16 manifestations of your consciousness (at least that is how I interpreted it). Each manifestation exists under a super-category of \[List the four categories\]. A skill check is a simple 2d6 roll, with a number to beat. Each point added to a manifestation will add +1 to the result of the roll. It was frustrating at times failing skill checks that were meant to progress the story (especially ones with an 70%+ chance of success), but hitting low probability checks was super satisfying. I also never felt like I was running out of things to do. There was always something new to discover, and with only one full playthrough, I know I've missed out on a lot of content.

## Overwatch

In February, Overwatch 2 dropped the 2 and released 5 new heroes. I used to play the HELL out of this game years ago, before Overwatch 2. Now that this huge update dropped, I thought, why not give it another shot? And all I can say is: damn, they've been cooking for a while now with this game.

When Overwatch 2 initially released, I played a little bit, but the 5v5 gameplay (which used to be 6v6) did not appeal to me. On top of that, Overwatch 1 cost $39.99 to purchase, while Overwatch 2 uses a free-to-play model, with the option to purchase skins and a battle pass for an extra. On the surface, that isn't a bad idea, a lower barrier to entry means more players. However, new heroes were locked behind the battle pass. You could pay to unlock it faster, or grind 20-40 hours to unlock it. Before, new heroes were given to everyone day 1. This was a dealbreaker for me. One of the best parts of Overwatch is getting to enjoy the new heroes they come up with. Instead, you have to play without it for weeks, or pay the battle pass fee to get it now.

A big part of the advertising for Overwatch 2 was the promise of PvE story missions. They already did a few for yearly events, but these were supposed to be bigger and better. Each hero had skill trees and could be leveled up to unlock new abilities. There were new enemies, new maps, and multiple story missions to play through. 6 months after the game released, all of this was scrapped. The only released one DLC with three missions for $15, with no skill trees. After that, it was confirmed by the devs that no more story missions would be released. It was a huge disappointment to me and many other fans of the PvE content.

Fast forward to February, and a lot has changed. New heroes are no longer locked behind the battle pass. Just like in Overwatch 1, new heroes are given to everyone day 1. During each game, heroes can level up and unlock perks: one minor, one major. Each perk has two choices which can passively or actively affect the the hero you're playing. It leads to all kinds of new skill expression and strategies. In my opinion, it is an incredible system and a huge step up from Overwatch 1. Every new hero added this year is also a lot of fun to play.

Even though no PvE story missions are being developed, and the main way to play the game is 5v5 (though 6v6 unranked and competitive modes are now available), the game is now so much fun to play.
